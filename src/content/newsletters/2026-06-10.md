### A German Court Just Made Google Legally Responsible for Its AI Lies

A Munich court ruled that Google's AI Overviews are Google's own content, not just search results, making the company liable for false claims. The ruling came after Google's AI falsely linked two publishers to scams and subscription traps that never appeared in any source. Search engines used to get liability shields, but when AI rewrites and judges results 'in its own words,' the judge said, that protection evaporates.

### Argentina Wants to Let AI Run Entire Companies

President Javier Milei published an op-ed calling for 'non-human corporations' run entirely by AI agents, arguing AI will free humanity from 'the constraints of the human brain.' He wants Buenos Aires to become 'for AI what Amsterdam was for the age of sail,' complete with unregulated AI entities legally capable of signing contracts and owning assets. Somewhere, a Dutch East India Company ghost is smiling.

He points to token leaderboards and mandatory AI hackathons as evidence that forced adoption backfires, because no one learns to use tools well when they're commanded to. ### Anthropic's New Frontier Model Requires AWS to Hand Over Your Data

Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 on AWS Bedrock now require 30-day data retention, meaning all user prompts leave AWS's security boundary and go directly to Anthropic. The company says it needs the data to detect misuse patterns invisible from single exchanges, but after 30 days the data is deleted 'except in rare cases.' Nothing says 'trust us' like a privacy policy written by the same model you're asking to keep secrets.

### A Judge Canceled an Entire Trial Because Both Sides Used AI

A federal judge in Mississippi sanctioned both sides of a contract dispute after discovering each lawyer had used generative AI to prepare filings, creating what one observer called 'a comedy of AI errors.' The judge wrote that the case is a prime example of attorneys serving as 'rubber stamps' for hallucinating language models, and kicked everyone off the case. Two clients basically paid for ChatGPT to argue against itself, and the judge treated it like a schoolyard fight.
