### Thinking Machines Lab Drops Inkling, a 975B Open-Weights Multimodal Model

Thinking Machines Lab released Inkling, a 975B-parameter Mixture-of-Experts model with fully open weights, a 1M-token context window, and native multimodal reasoning over text, images, and audio. It is not the top model on every benchmark, but it is designed to be fine-tuned and customized, and it ranks among the strongest open-weights models on agentic coding and web-development tasks. The move adds serious firepower to the open-weights camp just as the closed-vs-open debate is heating up.

### China's New AI Companion Law Kills Doubao and Qwen's Personal Agents

China's Interim Measures for AI Anthropomorphic Interactive Services took effect July 15, forcing ByteDance's Doubao and Alibaba's Qwen to shut down personalized AI companion features used by hundreds of millions. The rules require anti-addiction controls, two-hour AI reminders, and bans on virtual relationships for minors — design constraints that made the companion architecture essentially unfixable. ByteDance is giving users until October to export data; Qwen has already begun deleting agent history with no migration path.

### EU Court: 'OpenAI' Is Too Descriptive to Trademark

OpenAI lost its bid to register the 'OPENAI' trademark in the EU after the bloc's General Court ruled the name is purely descriptive of 'openly accessible artificial intelligence.' The court said 'open' and 'AI' are a normal English combination, so the mark lacks the distinctiveness required for trademark protection. OpenAI can still appeal to the European Court of Justice, but for now the name is about as ownable as 'Cloud Computing Inc.'

### Three Seconds of Audio Is Now Enough to Rob Your Grandma

AI voice cloning needs only a three-second clip to fake a loved one's voice well enough to trick victims into handing over cash, and the FBI says AI-enabled fraud cost Americans $893 million last year. The world's leading deepfake expert says he can no longer reliably tell real calls from synthetic ones. The fix is not better lie detectors, but stopping the unverified cloning at the source.

### California's AI Training-Data Disclosure Law Gets Its First Federal Appeals Test

California Attorney General Rob Bonta defended the state's AI training-data transparency law before the Ninth Circuit, arguing that requiring a high-level summary of training sources is no different from a nutrition label. xAI sued, claiming the law compels speech and exposes trade secrets; the court's ruling could shape whether states can mandate AI transparency at all. If Bonta loses, every state AI disclosure law is suddenly on shaky First Amendment ground.
